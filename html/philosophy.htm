Thoughts on Software Quality Assurance üí≠
  • I see my role as an observer and communicator, not as the gatekeeper or ‚Äòquality police.‚Äô I provide feedback, not roadblocks.
  • Quality is a team effort, not just a post-coding activity.
  • QA teams complement and enhance quality efforts, not replace them.
  • It's impractical to test every possible scenario. Additional defects may not justify the resources.
  • Not all parts of an application are equally important, so testing should focus on high-risk areas.
  • Not all bugs are worth fixing.
A Holistic Approach to Quality

My approach to quality assurance goes beyond finding bugs. I look at the application as a whole, identifying gaps in requirements and assessing not just functionality but the overall user experience. Often, I find areas where the code meets the technical specifications, but the spec itself didn't fully consider certain user perspectives or scenarios. My goal is to provide feedback that aligns with real-world usage, helping teams anticipate potential issues and improve the product for end users.

Work Style and Collaboration

I value thoughtful, independent work and can produce high-quality results in less stressful environments. I perform best in environments where I have the space to think things through and manage my tasks at a steady, efficient pace. I prefer fewer meetings and minimal distractions, allowing me to focus on delivering quality work.

Thoughts on Manual Testing üí≠

I find value in exploratory testing. It allows me to approach testing naturally, seeing the software through the eyes of an end user. This flexibility encourages usability and UX observations that might not be considered during structured tests. The value of human, exploratory testing.

Thoughts on Automation Testing üí≠

I'm all for automation but believe automation should be approached with caution. It is not a universal solution. I believe in a balance between manual and automated testing.

  • Automation doesn't eliminate the need for manual testing.
  • Not every test is a good candidate for automation.
  • Automation requires ongoing time investment (maintenance).
  • Automation scripts can grow too big and become unmanageable.

While test automation is valuable, it should be used strategically alongside manual testing.
